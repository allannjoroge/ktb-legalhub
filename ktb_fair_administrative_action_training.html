Fair Administrative Action Act

Cap. 7L — Giving effect to Article 47 of the Constitution

⏱ 30 min read 📝 10 quiz questions Pass mark: 70% 📅 Commenced 17 Jun 2015 👥 All KTB Staff
This module has been developed by the Directorate of Legal Services to help all KTB staff understand their obligations under Kenya's Fair Administrative Action Act (Cap. 7L). The module covers what fair administrative action means, how it applies to KTB's decision-making, and the rights of people affected by your decisions.

It includes five sections and a ten-question quiz. You must score at least 70% to pass.

Section 1 of 5
What Is the Fair Administrative Action Act?
Why does this matter to you personally? Every time you make a decision at KTB that affects someone else — declining a supplier application, terminating a contract, denying leave, disciplining a staff member, rejecting a sponsorship request, or removing someone from a database — you are taking an administrative action. This Act says that every person affected by your decisions has the constitutional right to be treated fairly, given reasons, and allowed to challenge those decisions. If you skip these steps, KTB's decision can be overturned by a court and you may have to start again from scratch.

The Fair Administrative Action Act (Cap. 7L) was enacted in 2015 to give effect to Article 47 of the Constitution, which guarantees every person the right to administrative action that is expeditious, efficient, lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair. It applies to every state and non-state body exercising administrative authority — including KTB.

In plain terms: whenever KTB (or any of its officers) exercises a power, performs a function, or makes a decision that affects someone's legal rights or interests, that action must be done fairly, with reasons, and with the opportunity for the affected person to be heard.

Who does this Act apply to?

Section 3 makes the Act very broad. It applies to any person exercising administrative authority, performing a judicial or quasi-judicial function, or whose action, omission, or decision affects the legal rights or interests of any person. This means it covers KTB as an institution, KTB's Board, and every individual KTB staff member who makes decisions affecting others.

The oversight body

The Commission on Administrative Justice (Office of the Ombudsman) is the body that receives complaints about administrative injustice. However, any aggrieved person can also go directly to the High Court for judicial review (Section 7).

Key Defined Terms

TermWhat It Means in Plain English
Administrative actionAny power, function, duty, act, omission, or decision by a person or body that affects someone else's legal rights or interests. (Section 2)
AdministratorThe person who takes the administrative action or makes the decision — at KTB, this could be any staff member, manager, committee, or the Board. (Section 2)
Empowering provisionThe law, rule, agreement, or instrument that gives you the authority to take the action. You must act within the boundaries of this provision. (Section 2)
DecisionAny administrative or quasi-judicial decision that is made, proposed to be made, or required to be made. A "failure" to make a decision (including a refusal) also counts. (Section 2)
Judicial reviewThe process by which a court examines whether an administrative decision was lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair — and can overturn it if it was not. (Sections 7–11)
Procedural fairnessThe requirement that the decision-maker follows a fair process — notice, hearing, reasons — not just reaches a fair outcome.

Commencement

The Act was assented to on 27 May 2015 and commenced on 17 June 2015. It applies to all proceedings pending at the time it came into force (Section 14). It operates alongside, not in place of, the common law rules of natural justice (Section 12).

How Does Fair Administrative Action Affect KTB?

KTB staff make administrative decisions every day. Many of these decisions affect the legal rights or interests of suppliers, partners, staff members, event participants, and the public. Each of these decisions must comply with the Act. Here are the KTB-specific situations where this law applies:

📋 Procurement Decisions
Disqualifying a bidder, debarring a supplier, terminating a procurement process, or rejecting a tender — each of these affects a person's legal rights and requires prior notice, reasons, and an opportunity to respond.
Section 4(3)(a)–(g)
👥 HR & Disciplinary Actions
Issuing warnings, suspending staff, denying promotions, terminating employment, or rejecting leave requests. The staff member has a right to notice, a hearing, and written reasons before any adverse action is taken.
Section 4(2)–(4)
🤝 Sponsorship & Partnership Decisions
Declining a hotel's request to be featured in KTB's marketing, rejecting a tour operator's partnership proposal, or removing a partner from KTB's preferred list. These decisions affect commercial interests and trigger fairness obligations.
Section 4(3)
📢 Public Notices & Policy Changes
When KTB changes a policy that affects the tourism industry — e.g. new criteria for tourism service provider listings, new event participation rules — it must issue public notice, invite views, and consider them before acting.
Section 5(1)(a)–(d)
🗃️ Database & Registration Decisions
Removing a travel agent from KTB's trade partner database, de-listing an accommodation provider, or refusing registration. The affected person has a right to know why and to challenge the decision.
Section 4(3), Section 6
💰 Financial & Contract Decisions
Withholding payment to a contractor, applying liquidated damages, terminating a contract for poor performance, or varying contract terms adversely. Each requires notice, reasons, and an opportunity for the affected party to respond.
Section 4(3)–(4)
🏛️ Board Decisions
Board resolutions that affect individuals — e.g. approving a staff restructuring, terminating the CEO's contract, or debarring a supplier — are administrative actions subject to fairness requirements.
Section 3, Section 4
📧 Complaints & Dispute Resolution
When KTB receives a complaint from a member of the public, a staff member, or a supplier and decides to reject it or take no action — that decision is also an administrative action requiring reasons.
Section 4(2), Section 6
🔍 Scenario — Think Before You Click "Reveal"
A tour operator who has participated in KTB-organised roadshows for the past three years is suddenly excluded from the next roadshow. When they enquire, a Marketing Manager tells them informally that they have been "blacklisted" but offers no written explanation or reason. The operator says they were never notified or given a chance to respond. Has KTB complied with the Fair Administrative Action Act?
Answer: No. Excluding a tour operator from KTB-organised roadshows adversely affects their commercial interests — this is an administrative action under Section 2. Under Section 4(3), KTB was required to: (a) give prior and adequate notice of the proposed exclusion and the reasons for it, (b) give the operator an opportunity to be heard and make representations, (d) provide a statement of reasons, and (g) share the information or evidence relied upon. The operator could apply to court for judicial review under Section 7, arguing procedural unfairness (s.7(2)(c)) and denial of a hearing (s.7(2)(a)(v)). A court could set aside the exclusion and direct KTB to reconsider after following the correct process (Section 11(1)(e)).
Section 3 of 5
Your Core Fair Administrative Action Obligations

Article 47 of the Constitution and Section 4 of the Act create personal obligations on every KTB officer who makes decisions affecting others. These are not optional courtesies — they are legally enforceable rights that people can take you to court over.

✅ DO

  • Give prior notice — before taking any action that will adversely affect someone, tell them what you intend to do and why (s.4(3)(a))
  • Allow a hearing — give the affected person an opportunity to be heard, make representations, and attend proceedings (s.4(3)(b), s.4(4)(a)–(b))
  • Provide written reasons — every person has the right to be given written reasons for any administrative action taken against them (s.4(2))
  • Inform them of appeal rights — tell the person about any right to review, internal appeal, or external challenge (s.4(3)(c))
  • Share the evidence — give the person the information, materials, and evidence you are relying on to make your decision (s.4(3)(g))
  • Allow cross-examination — let the affected person question people who give adverse evidence against them (s.4(4)(c))
  • Act within your authority — only make decisions you are legally empowered to make under the relevant law or instrument (s.7(2)(a)(i))
  • Respond to requests for reasons within 30 days — if someone asks why an action was taken, you must respond in writing within 30 days (s.6(3))

❌ DON'T

  • Don't act without notice — taking an adverse decision without telling the affected person first violates s.4(3)(a) and can be overturned
  • Don't deny a hearing — refusing to let someone respond before you decide is a ground for judicial review (s.7(2)(a)(v))
  • Don't act on bias — if you have a personal interest or pre-formed view, you should not be the decision-maker; bias is a ground for review (s.7(2)(a)(iv))
  • Don't act on someone else's direction — if you make a decision because someone unauthorised told you to, that is a ground for review (s.7(2)(g))
  • Don't ignore relevant facts — failing to consider relevant information is a separate ground for overturning your decision (s.7(2)(f))
  • Don't delay unreasonably — unreasonable delay in making a decision you are required to make is itself reviewable (s.7(2)(j))
  • Don't refuse to give reasons — if you fail to provide reasons when asked, the court will presume your decision was made without good reason (s.6(4))
  • Don't act disproportionately — your action must be proportionate to the interests or rights affected; an extreme response to a minor issue is reviewable (s.7(2)(l))
⚠️ Presumption against you: Section 6(4) creates a powerful legal presumption: if you fail to give reasons for your decision when asked, the court will presume your decision was taken without good reason. This means the burden shifts to KTB to prove the decision was justified. Always document your reasons at the time you make the decision — not after someone challenges it.
🔍 Scenario — Think Before You Click "Reveal"
The HR Manager decides to transfer a KTB staff member from the Nairobi office to a regional office. The staff member is told about the transfer on Friday afternoon and is expected to report to the new station on Monday. The staff member says this is unfair. Is it?
Answer: Yes, this likely violates the Act. A transfer that affects a staff member's working conditions and personal life is an administrative action under Section 2. Under Section 4(3), the employee should have received: (a) prior and adequate notice with reasons for the proposed transfer, (b) an opportunity to be heard and make representations (e.g., family circumstances, health issues), and (c) notice of any right to internal appeal. Two days' notice for a station transfer is unlikely to constitute "adequate" notice. The employee could apply to the High Court for judicial review, and a court could set aside the transfer under Section 11(1)(e) for being procedurally unfair (Section 7(2)(c)).

The 15 Grounds for Judicial Review (Section 7(2))

A court can overturn KTB's administrative decision on any of these grounds. Every KTB decision-maker should know them:

#GroundWhat It Means at KTB
1Not authorised (s.7(2)(a)(i))You made a decision you had no legal power to make
2Excess of jurisdiction (s.7(2)(a)(ii))You went beyond the scope of your delegated authority
3Unlawful delegation (s.7(2)(a)(iii))You acted on delegated power that was not lawfully given
4Bias (s.7(2)(a)(iv))You had a personal interest or pre-formed view — or could reasonably be suspected of it
5Denied a hearing (s.7(2)(a)(v))You didn't give the person a reasonable chance to state their case
6Procedural non-compliance (s.7(2)(b))You didn't follow a mandatory procedure set out in the empowering law
7Procedural unfairness (s.7(2)(c))The process you followed was unfair, even if not in breach of a specific rule
8Error of law (s.7(2)(d))Your decision was based on a misunderstanding of the law
9Ulterior motive (s.7(2)(e))You used your power for a purpose other than its intended one
10Ignored relevant facts (s.7(2)(f))You failed to consider information you should have considered
11Acted on unauthorised direction (s.7(2)(g))Someone not authorised by law told you to act, and you did
12Bad faith (s.7(2)(h))You acted dishonestly or with malicious intent
13Not rationally connected (s.7(2)(i))Your decision doesn't logically connect to its purpose, the law, the evidence, or your stated reasons
14Unreasonable / disproportionate (s.7(2)(k)–(l))The decision is excessive relative to the rights affected
15Violated legitimate expectations (s.7(2)(m))You broke a promise, established practice, or policy that the person reasonably relied on

Exhaustion of Remedies

Section 9(2) requires that all internal appeal mechanisms must be exhausted before going to court. This means KTB must actually have internal appeal or review processes — and must tell affected persons about them. If KTB has no internal process, a person can go straight to court.

Consequences, Remedies & Key Contacts

What Can a Court Do If KTB Gets It Wrong?

Under Section 11, the court has wide powers when it finds that KTB's administrative action was unlawful, unreasonable, or procedurally unfair:

Court OrderWhat It Means for KTB
Declare rights (s.11(1)(a))The court states what the parties' rights are — e.g. that the affected person had a right to be heard
Restrain KTB (s.11(1)(b))The court stops KTB from continuing the action that breached the law
Order reasons (s.11(1)(c))The court directs KTB to give reasons for its decision
Prohibit action (s.11(1)(d))The court bans KTB from acting in a particular manner
Set aside & remit (s.11(1)(e))The court cancels the decision and sends it back to KTB to redo it properly — with or without directions
Compel performance (s.11(1)(f))If KTB failed to act when it was required to, the court orders KTB to perform that duty
Temporary relief (s.11(1)(i))The court can grant an interim injunction stopping KTB from acting while the case is heard
Costs & compensation (s.11(1)(j))The court can order KTB to pay the applicant's legal costs and monetary compensation
⚠️ Real cost to KTB: When a court sets aside KTB's decision, KTB must redo the entire process — with notice, hearings, and reasons. This means delays to procurement, HR actions, and partnerships, plus legal fees and potential compensation payments. It also causes reputational damage. The cheapest and fastest route is always to follow fair process the first time.

What To Do When You Need to Make an Administrative Decision

Step-by-step response guide:
  1. Identify the action — is what you are about to do likely to adversely affect someone's legal rights or interests? If yes, this Act applies.
  2. Check your authority — confirm that you are authorised to make this decision under the relevant law, policy, or delegation.
  3. Give written notice — notify the affected person of the proposed action, the reasons for it, and the evidence you are relying on.
  4. Hold a hearing — give the person an opportunity to respond, in person or in writing, before you make your decision.
  5. Consider their response — genuinely consider the representations made. Document that you did so.
  6. Make your decision and give written reasons — record your decision, the reasons for it, and inform the person of their right to review or appeal.
  7. If in doubt, consult Legal — contact Allan Njoroge or Doreen Mutunga before taking any action you are unsure about.
🛡️ Section 12 — Natural Justice Still Applies: The Act is "in addition to and not in derogation from the general principles of common law and the rules of natural justice." This means that even if a specific requirement of the Act doesn't apply, the basic common-law duty to act fairly still does. When in doubt, err on the side of more process, not less.

5 Key Takeaways

1
Every decision that affects someone's rights triggers this Act. It doesn't matter how minor you think the decision is — if it adversely affects a person's legal rights or interests, you must follow fair process.
2
Notice → Hearing → Reasons → Appeal. This is the core sequence. Give notice, let the person respond, make your decision with written reasons, and tell them how to challenge it.
3
No reasons = no good reason. If you fail to give reasons when asked, the law presumes your decision was made without justification (Section 6(4)). Always document your reasoning at the time of the decision.
4
Courts have wide remedial powers. A court can cancel your decision, order KTB to redo the process, issue injunctions, and award costs and compensation. Prevention is far cheaper than litigation.
5
Internal mechanisms matter. KTB must have internal appeal processes and must tell affected persons about them. If there's no internal remedy, people go straight to court (Section 9(2)).
