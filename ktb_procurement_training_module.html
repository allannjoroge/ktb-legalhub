Section 1 of 5
What Is the Public Procurement Act?
Why does this matter to you personally? Every time you request goods, services, or works for KTB — whether it is stationery, a conference venue, a marketing agency, or a travel photographer — you are triggering a procurement process governed by this law. If you influence that process improperly, split a tender to avoid thresholds, or accept a gift from a bidder, you personally face up to 10 years in prison and a KES 4 million fine (Section 176). This is not just a rule for the procurement team — it is the law for every KTB staff member.

The Public Procurement and Asset Disposal Act (No. 33 of 2015) is Kenya's primary law governing how all government bodies — including state corporations like KTB — buy goods, hire services, commission works, and dispose of assets. It gives effect to Article 227 of the Constitution, which requires that public procurement be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective.

The Act replaced the older Public Procurement and Disposal Act of 2005. It is supplemented by the Public Procurement and Asset Disposal Regulations, 2020 (Legal Notice No. 69 of 2020), which provide the detailed procedures, thresholds, and forms that procuring entities must follow.

Who enforces this law?

The Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (PPRA), established under Section 8 of the Act, monitors, investigates, and enforces compliance. Disputes go to the Public Procurement Administrative Review Board (Section 27), and ultimately to the High Court.

Commencement & Application

The Act received Presidential assent on 18 December 2015 and commenced on 7 January 2016. It repealed the Public Procurement and Disposal Act, 2005 (No. 3 of 2005). The Act applies to all state organs and public entities, including KTB as a state corporation under the State Corporations Act (Cap. 446). Under Section 4, it covers procurement planning, procurement processing, inventory and asset management, disposal of assets, and contract management.

Section 2 of 5
How Does Procurement Law Affect KTB?

KTB is a state corporation established under the Tourism Act (Cap 383). Every KTB department triggers procurement obligations — from marketing campaigns to IT equipment, from event venues to consultancy services. Here are the specific ways this law touches KTB's work:

🎯 Marketing Campaigns & Advertising
Hiring advertising agencies, media buying, social media management, or brand ambassadors requires competitive tendering. You cannot simply re-appoint last year's agency without following procurement procedure.
Sections 91–92 (choice of procurement method)
🏨 Event & Conference Services
Booking venues, catering, AV equipment, and event management for tourism expos and trade shows — all must follow RFQ or open tender thresholds. Splitting a conference budget across multiple small orders to avoid thresholds is a criminal offence.
Section 54 (prohibition on splitting contracts)
📸 Photography & Content Creation
Commissioning photographers, videographers, or content creators for destination marketing. These are consultancy/service procurements that must follow Section 115–133 if advisory/intellectual in nature, or Part IX if non-consultancy services.
Sections 115–116 (consultancy procurement)
💻 IT Systems & Digital Platforms
Procuring website development, CRM systems, data analytics platforms, or cybersecurity services. Standardisation or compatibility with existing systems may justify additional procurement from the same supplier — but only under the strict conditions of Section 103(2)(d).
Section 103(2)(d) (direct procurement for standardisation)
🛫 Travel Trade Partnerships
Engaging travel agents, tour operators, or airline partners for joint promotions. If KTB is paying for these services using public funds, the Act applies. International partners may involve additional rules under Section 89 on international tendering.
Section 89 (international tendering)
📋 Board & Governance
Board members who have interests in companies bidding for KTB contracts must declare those interests and recuse themselves. Failure to do so makes the contract voidable, and the awarding officer must reimburse KTB for all costs. (Section 66(7))
Section 66 (conflict of interest in procurement)
🧹 Asset Disposal
Disposing of old vehicles, office furniture, IT equipment, or obsolete promotional materials. KTB must form a disposal committee (Section 163), follow prescribed disposal methods, and cannot sell assets to its own employees except as expressly allowed (Section 166).
Sections 163–166 (disposal of assets)
👥 AGPO Reservations (30%)
At least 30% of KTB's procurement budget must be reserved for youth, women, and persons with disability. The procurement plan must demonstrate this allocation and be submitted to PPRA within 60 days of the financial year. (Section 157(10))
Sections 53(6), 157(10) (preference & reservations)
🔍 Scenario — Think Before You Click "Reveal"
KTB's Marketing Director urgently needs a creative agency to produce content for an upcoming international tourism expo in 3 weeks. The budget is KES 2.8 million. The Director says: "We've worked with Agency X before, they know our brand — just give them the job directly. We don't have time for a tender." What should happen?
Answer: At KES 2.8 million for services, this falls within the Request for Quotations threshold (up to KES 3 million for services per the Second Schedule). The procurement team should issue an RFQ to at least 3 registered suppliers. Direct procurement under Section 103 is not justified here — the urgency was foreseeable (the expo date was known in advance), and Section 103(2)(b) specifically excludes urgency caused by the procuring entity's own delay. Familiarity with a supplier is not a lawful ground for direct procurement. The Marketing Director should have planned this procurement at the start of the financial year under Section 53(2).
Section 3 of 5
Your Core Procurement Obligations

The Act places obligations not just on KTB as an organisation, but on you as an individual. Section 45(5) states clearly: "all public officers or State officers involved in procurement or asset disposal processes shall bear responsibility for their actions and omissions." Here is what you must and must not do:

✅ DO

  • Plan ahead — ensure your department's procurement needs are in the annual procurement plan before the financial year begins (s.53(2))
  • Confirm budget — never request a procurement unless funds are confirmed in the approved budget (s.53(8))
  • Use standard documents — always use the standard tender documents issued by PPRA (s.58)
  • Declare conflicts — if you, your spouse, child, or business associate has any interest in a bidder, declare it in writing and step away from the process (s.66(5)–(6))
  • Sign the confidentiality form — before participating in any procurement committee, sign the confidentiality declaration (s.67(2))
  • Keep records for 6 years — all procurement documents must be maintained for at least 6 years after contract completion (s.68(1))
  • Report contract awards — ensure all awards are published on KTB's notice board and website within 14 days of signing (Reg. 128)
  • Respect the 30% AGPO allocation — actively ensure disadvantaged groups are included in your procurement plans (s.53(6), s.157(10))

❌ DON'T

  • Don't split contracts — structuring a purchase as multiple smaller ones to avoid thresholds is a criminal offence (s.54(1))
  • Don't influence evaluations — any attempt to sway the evaluation committee after bids are submitted is an offence leading to disqualification and disciplinary action (s.65)
  • Don't share confidential bid information — disclosing tender contents, evaluation details, or bid prices carries a 10-year debarment from government work (s.67(5))
  • Don't accept gifts from bidders — engagement in corrupt or fraudulent practices makes any resulting contract voidable (s.66(1)–(3))
  • Don't award contracts to yourself, spouse, or children — Section 59 prohibits this absolutely for officers of the procuring entity
  • Don't start procurement without a plan — an accounting officer who knowingly starts procurement without confirming the budget commits an offence (s.53(9))
  • Don't change tender prices after opening — the tender sum as read at opening is final and cannot be corrected, revised, or amended (s.82)
  • Don't approve retrospective procurement — no procurement approval may be backdated except for genuine urgent needs (s.69(2))
⚠️ Criminal Liability Warning: Under Section 176(2), any individual who commits a procurement offence faces a fine of up to KES 4 million or imprisonment for up to 10 years, or both. A body corporate faces a fine of up to KES 10 million. In addition, public officers face internal disciplinary action and may be required to personally reimburse KTB for any losses caused by their actions (Section 54(4), Section 66(7)).
🔍 Scenario — Think Before You Click "Reveal"
You are appointed to the evaluation committee for a KES 15 million tender for tourism promotion services. During evaluation, you realise that one of the bidding firms is partly owned by your brother-in-law. You didn't know this when you were appointed. The evaluation is already halfway done. What should you do?
Answer: Under Section 66(5), you must not take part in any further procurement proceedings related to this tender. Under Section 66(6), you must immediately disclose the conflict of interest in writing to the procuring entity (through the Accounting Officer). A brother-in-law's spouse is a "relative" under Section 66(11)(b) — "a child, parent, brother or sister of a spouse." If you continue participating and the contract is awarded to that firm, Section 66(7) makes the contract terminable, and the awarding officer must reimburse KTB for all costs incurred.

Contract Variations — Hard Limits

No price increase within 12 months — Section 139(3) prohibits any upward contract price variation within the first 12 months of signing. After 12 months, the maximum quantity variation for goods is 15%, and the cumulative price increase cannot exceed 25% of the original contract price. If variations would push the price above 25%, a fresh tender must be issued (Section 139(6)).

Performance Security

A winning bidder must submit performance security of up to 10% of the contract value before signing (Section 142). Per Regulation 135, this is required for contracts above KES 5 million for goods, works, and non-consultancy services. For AGPO categories (women, youth, persons with disability), the performance security is capped at 1% of the contract sum (Section 142(3)).

What To Do When Something Goes Wrong

Step-by-step response guide:
  1. Stop and report — if you become aware of any irregularity (bid manipulation, splitting, conflict of interest, bribery attempt), immediately report it to the Head of Procurement or the Legal Directorate. Do not try to fix it yourself.
  2. Document everything — write down what you observed, when, and who was involved. Preserve any emails, messages, or documents.
  3. Declare your own conflict — if you discover you have a personal conflict, disclose it in writing to the Accounting Officer immediately (s.66(5)–(6)).
  4. Do not destroy records — procurement records must be kept for 6 years (s.68). Destroying or concealing documents is obstruction under s.176(1)(a).
  5. Cooperate with investigators — PPRA investigators have the legal power to access all premises, books, records, and electronic documents (s.36). Obstruction is a criminal offence.
  6. Contact the Legal Directorate — Allan Njoroge and Doreen Mutunga are your first points of contact for any procurement compliance question.
🛡️ Protection from Personal Liability: Section 178 provides that you will not be personally liable for acts done in good faith in the performance of your duties. KTB will indemnify employees who are sued for actions taken in their official capacity, provided they were not guilty of gross negligence, misconduct, or grave abuse of office. The key is: follow the law, document your decisions, and report problems early.

5 Key Takeaways

1
Open tendering is the default. Every alternative method requires specific justification under the Act. If you're not sure which method applies, ask the Procurement Function.
2
Never split contracts to avoid thresholds. This is a criminal offence with up to 10 years imprisonment. If your genuine need exceeds an RFQ threshold, use open tender — don't break it into smaller pieces.
3
Declare conflicts early. If you, your spouse, child, parent, sibling, or in-law has any interest in a bidder, disclose it in writing and withdraw from the process. Silence makes you personally liable.
4
30% goes to AGPO. KTB must reserve at least 30% of its procurement budget for youth, women, and persons with disability. Tender security is waived for these groups — they use a Tender Securing Declaration instead.
5
You are personally responsible. Section 45(5) makes every officer involved in procurement personally responsible for their actions and omissions. Good faith + proper documentation = your best protection.
