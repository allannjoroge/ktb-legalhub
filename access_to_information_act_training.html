Foundation
What is the Access to Information Act?
🧑‍⚖️
Why this law matters to YOU personally
The Access to Information Act creates direct personal obligations on every KTB staff member — not just management. If you deliberately destroy a record to prevent its disclosure, you face criminal prosecution personally. If you are an information access officer and you refuse or delay a valid request, you are individually liable. This module explains exactly what you must and must not do.

What the Act is About

The Act gives effect to Article 35 of the Constitution, which guarantees every citizen the right to access information held by the State, or by another person where that information is needed to exercise or protect any right or fundamental freedom. It establishes the framework for how requests are made, processed and reviewed, and what information may lawfully be withheld.

The Act commenced on 21 September 2016 and applies to all public entities (including KTB) and to private bodies that receive public resources, perform public functions, or hold information of significant public interest.

⚖️
The Constitutional Principle: Duty to Disclose
Section 4(4) states the Act must be interpreted on the basis of a duty to disclose. Non-disclosure is only permitted in the specific circumstances exempted under section 6. KTB's default position must always be to disclose — not to withhold.

The Regulator

The Commission on Administrative Justice (CAJ) — also known as the Office of the Ombudsman — is the oversight and enforcement body. It designates one Commissioner as the "Access to Information Commissioner." CAJ decisions are binding on both national and county governments.

KTB Context
How Does This Law Apply to Us?
🏛️
KTB is a public entity — the Act applies in full
KTB is a state corporation established under the Tourism Act Cap. 383. Any citizen can request any record KTB holds. Every staff member must know when to assist, when to refer, and — critically — what they must never do when a request arrives.

Where the Act Touches KTB's Work

🏗️ Contracts & Procurement
Upon signing any contract, KTB must publish the scope of work, contract sum, supplier name and completion timeline on its website. Citizens may also request contract copies directly.
s.5(1)(e)
📊 Organisational Information
KTB must proactively publish its organisational structure, officer functions, salary grades, decision-making procedures and guidelines — and update them annually.
ss.5(1)(a), 5(1)(b)
📂 HR Records & Staff Files
Performance records, disciplinary files, payroll data and contracts are all "information" under the Act. A staff member may request their own personal information held by KTB.
ss.4, 13
📸 Events & Marketing Materials
Photographs, event attendee lists, marketing reports and campaign data created or commissioned by KTB are records that may be the subject of an access request.
ss.2, 4
💰 Budget & Expenditure
KTB's budget, expenditure reports, audit findings and financial statements are public records. Citizens may request these, and KTB should proactively publish key financial information.
ss.4, 5
📋 Board Decisions & Minutes
Board minutes and decisions may be requested. Some deliberations may qualify as exempt, but this must be assessed case by case under s.6 — it cannot simply be assumed.
ss.4, 6
📧 Official Correspondence
Official emails and reports created in the course of KTB's business are public records. A requester may ask for emails relating to a specific decision or contract.
ss.2, 17
🗄️ Records Management
Every unit must maintain accurate, authentic and organised records to facilitate access requests. KTB must also computerise its records management systems within 3 years of the Act applying to it.
s.17

Scenario — Marketing Department

Scenario
Journalist requests agency payment data
A journalist formally writes to KTB requesting a breakdown of all marketing agency payments in the last financial year — agency names, amounts paid, and campaigns supported. The Head of Marketing is uncomfortable releasing this, fearing it will generate a negative story. Can KTB refuse?
No — KTB cannot refuse on these grounds.
Under section 4(2), a citizen's right of access is not affected by the reason they give for requesting information, or KTB's belief about why they want it. The journalist's motive — even if critical — is legally irrelevant. KTB must assess the content against the specific s.6 exemptions only. Refusing because the information might be used negatively is not a lawful basis for withholding.

Scenario — HR Department

Scenario
Staff member requests their own performance appraisal
A KTB staff member formally applies to HR requesting a copy of their performance appraisal for the previous year, which they believe contains inaccurate assessments. HR is unsure whether they must provide it. What does the law require?
KTB must provide the information AND correct any inaccuracies.
Under section 4(1), every citizen has the right of access to information held by the State. The staff member's own performance record is personal information they are entitled to access. KTB must process the request within 21 days (s.9(1)). Under section 13, if the staff member demonstrates the record is inaccurate, KTB must correct, update or annotate it at its own expense within a reasonable time.

Core Obligations
What You Must and Must Not Do
⚠️
Criminal liability applies personally to you — not just to KTB
Certain breaches carry personal criminal liability. The most serious is deliberately destroying or hiding records to prevent disclosure (section 18). Study the table below carefully — these are your individual obligations.

Your Personal Obligations at a Glance

✓ DO
✓Refer any information request you receive to the designated Information Access Officer immediately
✓Maintain accurate, organised and retrievable records of all your official activities and decisions
✓Co-operate fully when the IAO requests records from your unit to respond to a request
✓Support proactive disclosure — publish KTB's organisational and contractual information on the website as required
✓Assess whether any information falls under a s.6 exemption before withholding, and document your reasons
✓Provide written reasons to any applicant whose request is refused and inform them of their right to appeal to the CAJ (s.9(4))
✓Transfer a misrouted application within 5 days and notify the applicant within 7 days (s.10)
✓Disclose information in good faith when lawfully directed — you are protected from criminal liability (s.28(9))
✓Report concerns about record tampering or improper withholding to your supervisor or the Legal Directorate
✗ DON'T
✗Handle an information request yourself without involving the Information Access Officer
✗Store official records in personal devices, personal email accounts or non-KTB systems
✗Alter, deface, block, erase, destroy or conceal any record once you know an information request has been made — this is a criminal offence (s.18)
✗Refuse to help a requester who cannot write due to illiteracy or disability — the IAO must help them reduce their request to writing (s.8(2))
✗Withhold information because you are uncomfortable with how it might be used or reported (s.4(2))
✗Charge any fee for submitting the application — only fees for reproduction/copying are permitted after approval (s.12(1))
✗Ignore a request or allow it to lapse — silence is a deemed refusal (s.9(6)) and the applicant can appeal immediately
✗Knowingly disclose exempt information — this carries up to Ksh 1,000,000 fine or 3 years' imprisonment (s.28(1))
✗Penalise or retaliate against a colleague who makes a good-faith public interest disclosure (s.16)

Scenario — Finance Department

Scenario
Finance officer deletes records after a request is received
KTB receives a formal information request from an NGO for all invoices and payment records for a specific event management contract. Two days later, a Finance Officer — concerned the records show procurement irregularities — permanently deletes the relevant files from the finance system. What is the legal position?
The Finance Officer has committed a criminal offence under section 18.
Section 18(1) makes it an offence for any officer of a public entity to alter, erase, destroy or conceal any record with the intention of preventing its disclosure after a request has been made. The officer is personally liable and faces up to Ksh 500,000 fine or 2 years' imprisonment (s.28(5)). The fact that the records may show irregularities does not justify deletion. The correct course is to raise concerns through proper whistleblower channels under section 16.
🛡️
Whistleblower protection (s.16)
Section 16 protects any KTB staff member who discloses information of public interest — including violations of law, mismanagement of funds, conflict of interest, corruption or dangers to public health — from dismissal, demotion or any form of penalisation. A disclosure to a law enforcement agency is deemed to be in the public interest. Any retaliation against you is actionable as a tort.

Fees — What Is Permitted

💳
Fee rules under section 12
KTB cannot charge any fee for receiving or submitting an information request (s.12(1)). Fees may only be charged to cover the actual cost of reproducing and supplying the information — for example, printing costs. Overcharging is a criminal offence under s.28(4)(a).

Annual Reporting Obligation (Section 27)

By 30 June of each year, KTB must submit a report to the CAJ covering the preceding year. The report must include:

1
Requests received and processed
The number of information requests KTB received and the number actually processed during the year.
2
Refusals and grounds
The number of determinations not to comply with requests and the main grounds for each refusal.
3
Processing times
The average number of days taken to process different types of requests.
4
Fees collected
The total amount of fees collected while processing requests during the year.
5
Staff and costs
Number of full-time staff devoted to processing requests and total expenditure incurred.

Proactive Disclosure — Ongoing Obligation

📢
KTB must publish proactively — citizens do not need to ask
KTB must keep updated on its website: its organisational structure and functions, officer duties and salary grades, decision-making procedures, guidelines, and a guide to the categories of information it holds. Upon signing any contract, KTB must also publish the scope of work, contract sum, supplier name and completion timeline (s.5(1)(e)).

🛡️
Good faith protection (s.28(9))
You are not criminally liable for disclosing or authorising a disclosure made in good faith in reliance on this Act. Follow KTB's proper procedures, consult Legal where in doubt, and act honestly — the law protects you.

What to Do When You Receive an Information Request

1
Do not handle it yourself
Pass the request immediately — in whatever form received — to the Information Access Officer or the Legal Directorate.
2
Log the date of receipt
The 21-day clock starts from the date KTB receives the request. Record the date accurately.
3
Preserve all relevant records
Do not delete, move, alter or destroy any potentially relevant record. Section 18 makes tampering a criminal offence.
4
Provide records when asked
If the IAO asks your unit to locate and provide records, co-operate promptly. Delays can cause KTB to breach the response deadline.
5
Do not contact the applicant directly
Do not communicate with the applicant unless specifically instructed to do so by the IAO.
6
If withholding, give reasons in writing
If a decision is made to withhold, the applicant must be informed in writing of the reasons and their right to appeal to the CAJ (s.9(4)).
7
If in doubt, consult Legal
If you are unsure whether information is exempt, consult the Legal Directorate before withholding anything.

5 Key Takeaways

1
The default is to disclose. KTB must withhold only where a specific s.6 exemption applies — discomfort is not a legal basis for withholding.
2
The 21-day deadline is strict. A missed deadline is automatically a deemed refusal — the applicant can go straight to the CAJ without any further notice.
3
Destroying a record after a request is a criminal offence carrying up to Ksh 500,000 fine or 2 years' imprisonment — even if you think the content is embarrassing.
4
The applicant's motive is irrelevant. You cannot refuse information because you dislike the reason for the request or think it will be used negatively.
5
Whistleblowing is legally protected. If you report corruption, mismanagement or rights violations in good faith, you cannot be lawfully dismissed or penalised for doing so.
