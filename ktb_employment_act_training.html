This module has been developed by the Directorate of Legal Services to help all KTB staff understand their obligations under the Employment Act (Cap. 226). It covers employment contracts, wages, rights and duties, termination procedures, and the protection of children in employment. It includes five sections and a ten-question quiz. You must score at least 70% to pass.

Reviewed and approved by Allan Njoroge, Director of Legal Services & Corporation Secretary, Kenya Tourism Board.

Section 1 — Foundation
What is the Employment Act and why does it matter to you?
Why this matters to you personally: The Employment Act defines the legal framework for your work at KTB. It protects your wages, your right to leave, and your right to fair treatment — and it sets out the obligations you and KTB owe each other. Violating this Act is not just an HR issue; it can lead to criminal charges, fines, and imprisonment for individual employees and managers alike.

What is the Employment Act?

The Employment Act (Cap. 226) is a law of Kenya that declares and defines the fundamental rights of employees, provides basic conditions of employment, regulates the employment of children, and provides for related matters. It was assented to on 22 October 2007 and commenced on 2 June 2008. It applies to all employees under a contract of service, and it binds the Government — meaning KTB as a state corporation is fully subject to its provisions (Section 3(3)).

Enforcement Body

The Act is enforced by Labour Officers (including the Commissioner of Labour and designated officers) and the Employment and Labour Relations Court, which has exclusive jurisdiction over employment disputes (Section 86(2)). The Cabinet Secretary for Labour oversees administration of the Act.

Legislative History

Commencement: 2 June 2008 (LN 61 of 2008). The Act repealed and replaced the previous Employment Act. It has been amended multiple times — most recently by the Statute Law (Miscellaneous Amendments) Act 2024 on 26 April 2024. Significant additions include pre-adoptive leave (Act No. 2 of 2021) and data protection obligations for child employment registers (reflecting the Data Protection Act Cap. 411C).
Section 2 — KTB Context
How the Employment Act applies specifically to KTB's operations
KTB is a state corporation established under the Tourism Act (Cap. 383). The Employment Act binds the Government and all state corporations — making it fully applicable to KTB, all its staff, managers, HR officers, and the Board. Every KTB employment relationship is governed by this Act as a minimum standard.

Scenario — Casual Worker Conversion

📌 Scenario: The Events Casual Worker
KTB hires David as a casual event steward for the annual Kenya Tourism Expo. The event runs for 6 weeks. David works every day for the entire 6 weeks. At the end, KTB pays him daily rates and thanks him for his service — no notice, no additional entitlements. Has KTB complied with the Employment Act?

No — KTB has not complied. Under Section 37(1)(a), when a casual employee works continuously for a period amounting to not less than one month, the contract is automatically deemed to be one where wages are paid monthly and Section 35(1)(c) applies — requiring 28 days' notice of termination. David worked 6 weeks, well over one month. KTB was required to give him proper written notice or payment in lieu. Simply paying daily rates and dismissing him is unlawful.

Scenario — Overseas Assignment

📌 Scenario: The London Marketing Posting
KTB assigns Grace, a Marketing Officer, to the KTB London office for 3 months. Her existing contract says nothing about overseas assignments. Grace asks HR what currency she will be paid in and what happens when she returns. HR says "your normal contract applies."

HR's response is non-compliant. Under Section 10(3)(f), where an employee works outside Kenya for more than one month, the written contract must specify: (i) the period of overseas work, (ii) the currency of payment while abroad, (iii) any additional remuneration or benefits, and (iv) terms for the employee's return to Kenya. These must be provided before Grace leaves. KTB HR must issue a written addendum to Grace's contract covering all four points prior to her departure.
Section 3 — Core Obligations & Principles
Personal obligations that apply to every KTB staff member
These obligations are personal — they apply to you as an individual employee, manager, or HR officer, not just to KTB as an institution. Acting contrary to these obligations may expose you personally to disciplinary action or criminal liability.

⚠️ Criminal Liability Alert — These acts carry personal criminal liability:
  • Using or assisting in forced labour — fine up to Ksh 500,000 or 2 years imprisonment (Section 4(3))
  • Failure to provide written employment particulars / pay slips — fine up to Ksh 100,000 or 2 years imprisonment (Section 16(4))
  • Wilfully failing to pay wages — fine up to Ksh 100,000 or 2 years imprisonment (Section 17(10))
  • Unlawful deductions not remitted to beneficiary — fine up to Ksh 100,000 or 2 years imprisonment (Section 19(5))
  • Making false entries in employment records — fine up to Ksh 100,000 or 6 months imprisonment (Section 75)
  • Child death or injury due to employer breach of child labour provisions — fine up to Ksh 500,000 or 12 months imprisonment (Section 65)

Scenario — Performance Dismissal

📌 Scenario: The Instant Dismissal
A KTB supervisor tells a junior procurement officer on a Friday afternoon: "Your work has been poor. You're dismissed effective immediately. Clear your desk." The employee is given no warning letter, no hearing, and no explanation of what was wrong. The supervisor tells HR it was "obvious" the employee wasn't performing.

This dismissal is unlawful and unfair. Section 41(1) requires that before terminating for poor performance, the employer must explain the reason in a language the employee understands and allow the employee to have a colleague or union representative present. Section 41(2) requires the employer to hear the employee's representations. No warning letters, no hearing, and no explanation means KTB has not followed fair procedure. Under Section 45(2)(c), termination in the absence of fair procedure is unfair. The employee can file a complaint within 3 months (Section 47), and the Employment and Labour Relations Court can order reinstatement or up to 12 months' gross salary in compensation (Section 49(1)(c)).

Redundancy — All Steps Required (Section 40)

When KTB needs to declare redundancies, ALL of the following must be completed:
  1. Notify the relevant trade union AND the area Labour Officer at least 1 month before the intended redundancy date (for union members).
  2. For non-union employees, notify the employee personally in writing AND the Labour Officer.
  3. Select employees using fair criteria: seniority, skill, ability and reliability.
  4. Pay any outstanding annual leave in cash to each affected employee.
  5. Give at least 1 month's notice or 1 month's wages in lieu of notice.
  6. Pay severance of at least 15 days' pay for each completed year of service.

Probationary Contracts — Key Rules (Section 42)

A probationary contract must be in writing. The period must not exceed 6 months — but may be extended with the employee's written agreement for a further 6 months (maximum aggregate: 12 months). No employee may be kept on probation beyond this. Either party may terminate with just 7 days' notice (or 7 days' wages in lieu). The full hearing requirements of Section 41 do not apply to probationary terminations.

Scenario — Redundancy Process

📌 Scenario: The Restructure
KTB undergoes an organisational restructure. Three positions in the Events department are declared redundant on a Monday. By Wednesday, the three staff members are called in, told their positions no longer exist, handed letters effective immediately, and paid only their final month's salary. No union was notified.

KTB has failed to comply with Section 40 on multiple counts. The affected employees were members of a union, so KTB was required to notify the union AND the Labour Officer at least one month before the termination date — not two days. Outstanding annual leave was not paid in cash. One month's notice (or payment in lieu) was not provided. Severance at 15 days per completed year of service was not paid. All three terminations are therefore unlawful. KTB faces potential reinstatement orders or compensation of up to 12 months' gross salary per employee, plus exposure to severance claims.

Remedies for Unfair Termination (Section 49)

Where a labour officer finds a dismissal unjustified, they may recommend the employer pay the affected employee:
  • Wages for the notice period the employee was entitled to
  • Proportionate wages for work done before dismissal
  • Up to 12 months' gross salary as compensation
  • Reinstatement or re-engagement in comparable work at the same wage

Step-by-Step: What to Do When a Problem Arises

  1. Stop immediately. If you suspect an employment practice may breach the Act, pause before proceeding and seek advice.
  2. Contact the Legal Directorate. Reach Allan Njoroge or Doreen Mutunga for immediate guidance on any employment dispute or potential breach.
  3. Document everything. Ensure the employment file is complete — written contract, warning letters, hearing notes, correspondence. The burden of proof in disputes rests on the employer (Section 43).
  4. Follow prescribed procedures. For disciplinary matters, use the Section 41 process. For redundancy, follow Section 40 in full.
  5. If a complaint is filed, respond promptly to the Labour Officer. All Employment and Labour Relations Court proceedings should be managed through the Legal Directorate.
  6. Remediate promptly. If wages have been wrongfully withheld or deducted, repay them immediately. Courts order repayment in addition to fines (Section 25).

Key Takeaways

  • 1Every KTB employee on a contract of 3+ months must have a written contract with all prescribed particulars — issued within 2 months of starting.
  • 2Wages must be paid in full, in Kenyan shillings, on a working day. Lawful deductions must never exceed two-thirds of wages.
  • 3No dismissal for misconduct or poor performance is lawful without a prior hearing, in the employee's language, with the right to representation.
  • 4Redundancy without proper notice, fair selection, paid-out leave and 15 days' severance per completed year of service is unlawful.
  • 5Employment Act violations carry personal criminal liability — individual managers and HR officers can be fined or imprisoned, not only KTB as an organisation.
