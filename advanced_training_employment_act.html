Section 1 of 8
Contracts of Service — Formation & Particulars
The Employment Act applies to all employees employed under a contract of service (s.3(1)). It binds the Government (s.3(3)). KTB, as a state corporation, is fully bound. The terms in this Act are minimum terms — any agreement to relinquish them is null and void (s.3(6)).

When Must a Contract Be Written? (Section 9)

A contract must be in writing if it is for 3 months or more, or if the work cannot reasonably be completed within 3 months (s.9(1)). The employer is responsible for having it drawn up (s.9(2)). If the employee is illiterate or cannot understand the language, the employer must have it explained in a language the employee understands (s.9(4)).

Deadline: Particulars must be given within 2 months of employment start (s.10(1)). Changes must be communicated within 1 month (s.13(3)(a)). Records kept for 5 years after termination (s.10(6)).

Disciplinary Rules Statement (Section 12)

Required for employers with 50+ employees (s.12(3)). Must specify: applicable disciplinary rules, person to whom employee may appeal disciplinary decisions, and steps for grievance redress (s.12(1)).

Clearance Certificates (Section 9(5)–(9))

An employer must not require clearance/compliance certificates unless intending to enter into a contract of service (s.9(5)). Relevant entities must issue or reject the certificate within 7 days and must not charge a fee (s.9(8)).

Burden of proof: If the employer fails to produce a written contract or written particulars in legal proceedings, the burden of proving or disproving any alleged term falls on the employer (s.10(7)). Failure to provide written particulars is an offence: fine up to KES 100,000 or 2 years imprisonment or both (s.16(4)).
🔍 Practitioner Scenario
A KTB staff member has been working for 4 months but has never received written employment particulars. They now have a dispute about their leave entitlement. HR says "we agreed verbally that you get 14 days." Who bears the burden of proof?
KTB bears the burden. Section 10(7) — if the employer fails to produce written particulars, the burden of proving or disproving an alleged term is on the employer. Since KTB failed to provide written particulars within the 2-month deadline (s.10(1)), KTB must prove the 14-day agreement existed. The statutory minimum of 21 days (s.28(1)(a)) applies as the floor. HR should have issued written particulars within 2 months.
Section 2 of 8
Discrimination & Sexual Harassment

Prohibited Grounds of Discrimination (Section 5)

No employer shall discriminate directly or indirectly, or harass an employee or prospective employee on grounds of: race, colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, nationality, ethnic or social origin, disability, pregnancy, marital status, or HIV status (s.5(3)(a)).

Discrimination covers: recruitment, training, promotion, terms and conditions, termination, and all other matters arising from employment (s.5(3)(b)).

Reversed burden of proof: In any discrimination proceedings, the employer bears the burden of proving that discrimination did not take place (s.5(7)). This means KTB must show the decision was not based on a prohibited ground — the employee does not need to prove it was.

Equal Pay (Section 5(5))

An employer shall pay employees equal remuneration for work of equal value. This applies across gender, disability, and all other prohibited grounds.

What Is NOT Discrimination (Section 5(4))

ExceptionDetail
Affirmative actionMeasures consistent with promoting equality or eliminating discrimination (s.5(4)(a))
Inherent job requirementsDistinguishing based on an inherent requirement of the job (s.5(4)(b))
National employment policyEmploying a citizen in accordance with national policy (s.5(4)(c))
State securityRestricting access to limited categories of employment for state security (s.5(4)(d))

Sexual Harassment Policy (Section 6)

Employers with 20 or more employees must issue a sexual harassment policy statement (s.6(2)). The policy must contain: the definition of sexual harassment as in s.6(1), a statement that every employee is entitled to work free of harassment, that the employer will take disciplinary action, how complaints may be made, and that confidentiality will be maintained (s.6(3)).

KTB must bring the policy to the attention of every person under its direction (s.6(4)).

🔍 Practitioner Scenario
During recruitment for a marketing position, a KTB interview panellist asks a female candidate whether she is planning to have children in the next two years. The candidate is not selected. She files a discrimination complaint. What are the issues?
Multiple issues. (1) Asking about pregnancy plans constitutes discrimination on grounds of sex/pregnancy under s.5(3)(a). (2) The question relates to recruitment, which is expressly covered (s.5(3)(b)). (3) Under s.5(7), the burden is on KTB to prove the non-selection was not based on the pregnancy-related question. (4) This could also amount to unfair termination of the recruitment process under s.46(a) — pregnancy is a listed unfair reason. HR must train all panellists to avoid prohibited questions.

Protection of Wages & Deductions

When Are Wages Due? (Section 18)

Casual employees: end of day. Employees paid at intervals <1 month: end of that period. Monthly employees: end of each month. Upon summary dismissal: all moneys due paid on dismissal date (s.18(4)). On termination by effluxion of time: entire amount payable immediately (s.18(5)(a)). On dismissal: employer must deliver written report to labour officer within 7 days (s.18(5)(b)).

Lawful Deductions (Section 19)

TypeCondition

Absence without leaveMax 1 day's wages per working day absent (s.19(1)(c))

Employee-requestedIn writing; employer has no beneficial interest (s.19(1)(g))
Loan repaymentWritten agreement; max 50% of wages after other deductions (s.19(1)(h))
Total deduction cap: All deductions combined must not exceed two-thirds of the employee's wages at any one time (s.19(3)). Employer advance recovery is capped at 1 month's wages (or 2 months for written contracts) (s.17(9)). Failure to remit deductions: fine up to KES 100,000 or 2 years imprisonment; court may order refund to employee + payment to beneficiary from employer's own funds (s.19(5)–(6)).

Itemised Pay Statement (Section 20)

Must be given at or before each wage payment. Must show: gross amount, all deductions and their purposes, and method of payment (s.20(2)). Does not apply to casuals, piece-rate workers, or employment <6 months (s.20(3)).

Key HR point: A female employee must not forfeit annual leave on account of having taken maternity leave (s.29(7)). Annual leave and maternity leave are independent entitlements. Maternity leave can be extended with employer consent, or followed immediately by sick leave or annual leave (s.29(3)).
🔍 Practitioner Scenario
A KTB female employee returns from 3 months maternity leave and immediately asks to take her 21 days annual leave. HR says she has "already been away for 3 months" and denies the leave. Is HR correct?
HR is wrong. Section 29(7) is explicit: no female employee shall forfeit her annual leave entitlement under s.28 on account of having taken maternity leave. These are separate statutory entitlements. Denying the annual leave could constitute discrimination on grounds of pregnancy/sex (s.5(3)(a)) and potentially unfair treatment under s.46(a)–(b) (pregnancy and leave-related reasons are prohibited grounds for discipline or adverse action).
Section 5 of 8
Termination & Notice Requirements

Notice Periods (Section 35)

Pay FrequencyNotice Required
Daily wages (casual)Terminable at the close of any day without notice (s.35(1)(a))
Less than monthlyEnd of the next period following written notice (s.35(1)(b))
Monthly or more28 days written notice (s.35(1)(c))
Contract with longer noticeContract terms prevail if more favourable (s.35(2))

Payment in lieu of notice is permitted (s.36). If an employee gives notice and the employer waives it, the employer must pay for the unserved notice period (s.38).

Casual to Term Conversion (Section 37)

A casual employee who works continuously for 1+ month, or whose work cannot reasonably be completed within 3 months, is automatically deemed to be on a monthly contract (s.37(1)). After 2 continuous months from original engagement, they are entitled to all statutory benefits as if they had never been casual (s.37(3)).

Probation (Section 42)

Maximum probation: 6 months, extendable by up to 6 further months with employee agreement (s.42(2)). Total cannot exceed 12 months (s.42(3)). Notice to terminate probation: 7 days or 7 days' wages in lieu (s.42(4)). Section 41 hearing requirements do not apply to probation terminations (s.42(1)).

Service Pay (Section 35(5))

An employee whose monthly contract is terminated under s.35(1)(c) is entitled to service pay for every year worked — unless they are a member of a registered pension/provident fund, a CBA gratuity scheme, NSSF, or a more favourable employer scheme (s.35(6)).

Section 6 of 8
Unfair Termination & Summary Dismissal

Pre-Termination Hearing (Section 41)

Mandatory for misconduct, poor performance, or physical incapacity. Before terminating, the employer must: (1) explain to the employee in a language they understand the reason for the proposed termination, (2) allow the employee to have another employee or shop-floor union rep present, (3) hear and consider any representations the employee (and their representative) may make (s.41(1)–(2)). This does NOT apply to probation terminations (s.42(1)).

Unfair Termination — Prohibited Reasons (Section 46)

The following are never fair reasons for dismissal or discipline:

❌ Never Fair Reasons

  • Pregnancy or any pregnancy-connected reason (s.46(a))
  • Taking or proposing to take lawful leave (s.46(b))
  • Trade union membership or proposed membership (s.46(c))
  • Trade union activities outside working hours (or with consent, within) (s.46(d))
  • Acting as a union officer or workers' representative (s.46(e))
  • Refusing to join or withdrawing from a union (s.46(f))
  • Race, colour, tribe, sex, religion, political opinion, nationality, social origin, marital status, HIV status, or disability (s.46(g))
  • Filing a complaint or legal proceedings against employer (unless irresponsible and without foundation) (s.46(h))
  • Participating in a lawful strike (s.46(i))

✅ Fair Termination Requires

  • A valid reason (s.45(2)(a))
  • Reason related to conduct, capacity, compatibility, or operational requirements (s.45(2)(b))
  • Fair procedure followed (s.45(2)(c))
  • 13+ months continuous employment before unfair termination claim arises (s.45(3))
  • Employer bears burden of proving reason for termination (s.43(1))
  • If employer fails to prove, termination is deemed unfair (s.43(1))

Certificate of Service (Section 51)

Must be issued upon termination (unless employment was <4 weeks). Must contain: employer name/address, employee name, commencement date, nature and place of employment, cessation date. Employer is not obliged to give a character reference (s.51(3)). Failure or false statement: fine up to KES 100,000 or 6 months imprisonment (s.51(4)).

Section 8 of 8
Disputes, Penalties & Limitation Periods

Dispute Resolution (Section 86)

Any dispute about a contract of service — neglect, refusal to fulfil, questions of rights/liabilities, misconduct, ill treatment, or injury — may be brought to a labour officer or lodged in the Employment and Labour Relations Court (ELRC) (s.86(1)). No other court has jurisdiction (s.86(2)).
