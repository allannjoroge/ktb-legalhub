Before you start
The Conflict of Interest Act 2025 commenced on 19 August 2025 and repealed the old Public Officer Ethics Act. It introduces new obligations for all public officers at KTB — including stricter rules on gifts, outside employment, decision-making, and post-employment restrictions. This module has 5 learning sections and a 10-question quiz.
1
Foundation
What is the Conflict of Interest Act?
🆕
This is a new law — the old rules have changed
The Conflict of Interest Act 2025 (Act No. 11 of 2025) commenced on 19 August 2025 and fully repealed the Public Officer Ethics Act (Cap. 185B). If you have been following the old rules, you need to read this module carefully — several obligations are stricter under the new Act.

A conflict of interest arises when a public officer's private interests could improperly influence — or could reasonably be seen to influence — the way they perform their official duties. The Act covers three situations: where a conflict actually exists, where it could reasonably appear to exist, and where it could arise in the future.

The Act is administered by the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (EACC), which has powers to investigate, recommend disciplinary action, and refer matters to the Director of Public Prosecutions. For KTB as a state corporation, your responsible Commission is the Public Service Commission.

Key DefinitionPlain English Meaning
Conflict of interestWhen your private interests could improperly affect how you do your job
Private interestAny personal benefit, privilege, or advantage you or your family could gain from your position
Public officerAll KTB staff — as defined under Article 260 of the Constitution
Reporting entityKTB — as a state corporation under Cap. 446, KTB is a reporting entity
Significant official dealingAny engagement with an entity that could be seen to influence your decisions — supplying goods, giving advice, having oversight
Complimentary treatmentFree travel, hospitality, training, scholarship or medical treatment offered by someone who deals with KTB
💡
The appearance test
You do not have to actually benefit from a conflict for it to be a violation. If a reasonable person looking at the situation from outside would think your judgment could be influenced by a private interest — that is enough. Always ask yourself: "How would this look if it was reported in a newspaper?"
2
KTB Context
How does this affect KTB specifically?

KTB engages daily with suppliers, tourism operators, media agencies, event companies, consultants, and government bodies. Many of these relationships create opportunities for conflicts to arise — sometimes without staff realising it. Here are the situations most likely to arise at KTB:

🛒 Procurement decisions
Participating in evaluating or awarding a tender to a company where you, a family member, or a close associate has an interest.
Section 10, 19 — conflict in decision-making and contracts
🎁 Gifts from suppliers
Receiving gifts, hospitality, complimentary hotel stays, air tickets, or any other benefit from a KTB supplier or tourism partner.
Section 16, 17, 18 — gifts and complimentary treatment
💼 Side businesses
Running or having a financial interest in a consultancy, tour operator, hotel, or agency that has or could have dealings with KTB.
Section 19, 20, 23 — contracts and restricted employment
👥 Recruitment
Being involved in a recruitment or selection process where a family member, relative, or close associate is a candidate.
Section 21 — conflict in recruitment
📢 Job offers while in post
Being approached for employment by a company or organisation that has dealings with KTB, and not disclosing or allowing it to influence your decisions.
Section 15 — offers of outside employment
📊 Use of official information
Using non-public information obtained through your KTB role to benefit yourself, a family member, or another person — for example in business decisions.
Section 13 — misuse of official information
📌 ScenarioThe Tourism Partner Invitation

A hotel chain that has a contract with KTB invites a senior marketing officer for a complimentary three-night stay at their new Mombasa property, saying it will help the officer "understand the product better." The officer is also involved in reviewing KTB's marketing contracts. Should the officer accept?

✗ No — this is complimentary treatment from a party with significant official dealings with KTB.
Section 18 prohibits accepting complimentary treatment unless it is required in an official capacity or in exceptional circumstances. A hotel with a KTB contract qualifies as having significant official dealings. The officer must decline or, if there is a genuine official reason, declare the acceptance within 48 hours in writing to the reporting authority. Given the officer's role in contract review, this creates an obvious appearance of conflict regardless of intent.
3
Your Legal Obligations
What the Act requires you to do

The Act imposes specific obligations on every KTB staff member. These are not optional or aspirational — they are legal requirements with criminal penalties for breach.

✅ You MUST do these
✓Actively avoid any real, apparent, or potential conflict of interest
✓Disclose any private interest that affects your official duties immediately
✓Recuse yourself from any discussion or decision where you have a conflict
✓Declare any gift received within 48 hours — even if you did not keep it
✓Report any job offer from an entity that deals with KTB within 7 days
✓Submit your declaration of income, assets and liabilities on time
✓Get permission before taking any other paid employment
❌ You must NEVER do these
✗Make a decision that affects an entity where you have a private interest
✗Accept gifts, cash, or complimentary treatment from suppliers or contractors
✗Be a party to or beneficiary of any KTB contract — in your own name or through a family member
✗Use your KTB position to influence decisions for personal benefit
✗Participate in recruitment where a relative or associate is a candidate
✗Use non-public KTB information for personal or business gain
✗Conceal a conflict of interest or collude with another officer to do so
⚠️
The gifts rule — read this carefully
You may never accept a gift from anyone who has an interest in KTB's decisions, carries on regulated activities with KTB, or has a contractual relationship with KTB — regardless of the value of the gift. The only exception is gifts received in an official protocol capacity that are non-monetary and within customary standards. Even then, you must declare within 48 hours. This applies to gifts received by your family members too — if you know a family member has received a gift from a KTB stakeholder, you must declare it.
📌 ScenarioThe Procurement Officer's Company

A KTB procurement officer's spouse runs a printing company. KTB issues a tender for branded promotional materials. The officer argues they will recuse themselves from the evaluation — so there is no problem. Is the recusal enough?

✗ No — recusal alone is not sufficient here.
Section 19 prohibits a public officer from being a party to or beneficiary of a contract with their reporting entity — and this extends to contracts with entities in which a public officer has a private interest. The spouse's company should not bid on the tender at all. Additionally, the officer must have disclosed this interest in their conflict of interest register. The recusal must be formally recorded and transmitted to the Commission within 60 days (Section 30). Simply stepping away informally is not enough.

📋
What must be registered as an interest
The Second Schedule lists registrable interests including: directorships in any company, shareholdings, land and property, contracts for supply of goods or services, plans for future employment, gifts and hospitality, sponsorships, overseas travel paid for by external parties, pending civil or criminal cases, and dual citizenship. If in doubt about whether something needs to be registered, contact the Legal Directorate.
🚨
Post-employment restrictions — these apply after you leave KTB
For two years after leaving KTB, you may not: act for or against KTB in any matter you previously handled; accept appointment to a board or employment with a company that had significant official dealings with KTB in your last two years; or represent any person before KTB. Breaching these restrictions is a criminal offence under Section 27.
5
Penalties & Response
Consequences, protection, and what to do

The Act carries serious penalties. Unlike the old Public Officer Ethics Act, the Conflict of Interest Act 2025 introduced substantially heavier sanctions to reflect the seriousness of the offences.

Criminal penalties
Individuals: Up to KSh 4 million fine and/or up to 10 years imprisonment.
Companies: Up to KSh 10 million fine.
Plus: A mandatory additional fine equal to twice any benefit gained or loss caused.
Additional consequences
Suspension from office during investigation (up to 90 days). Disciplinary action by KTB. Referral to the DPP for prosecution. Forfeiture of undeclared or unexplained assets. Public identification as a violator.

What to do if you find yourself in a conflict situation:

1
STOP — do not proceed with the decision or action
As soon as you realise you may have a private interest in a matter, stop participating immediately. Do not vote, sign, advise, or influence the outcome.
2
RECUSE — formally step back
Inform your supervisor or the chairperson of the relevant meeting that you are recusing yourself and state the nature of your interest. This must be recorded in the minutes.
3
DECLARE — notify the reporting authority in writing
Submit a written declaration of your private interest and the recusal to the Legal Directorate. KTB must transmit this to the Commission within 60 days.
4
CONTACT LEGAL — when in doubt
If you are unsure whether a situation creates a conflict, contact the Legal Directorate before acting. The Act protects people who act in good faith — but only if they take proper steps.
🛡️
Whistleblower protection
If you report a suspected conflict of interest by a colleague, the Act protects you from dismissal, discrimination, demotion, or any form of reprisal under Section 47. Such protection is enforceable as a civil claim. You should report concerns to the Legal Directorate or directly to the EACC.

5 things to remember:

1
It is not just about actual benefit — the appearance of conflict is enough to trigger the Act.
2
Declare early and often — late or missing declarations are criminal offences, not just administrative failures.
3
Gifts from KTB stakeholders are almost always prohibited — when in doubt, decline and contact Legal.
4
Recuse formally — stepping back informally is not enough. It must be recorded in minutes and reported to the Commission.
5
Post-employment restrictions are real — the Act follows you for two years after you leave KTB.
